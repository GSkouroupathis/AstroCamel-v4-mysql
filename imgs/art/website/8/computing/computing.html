Quantum Computing

"I'm not happy with all the analyses that go with just the classical theory, because nature isn't classical, dammit, and if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical, and by golly it's a wonderful problem, because it doesn't look so easy." Richard Feynman, 1982

Richard Feynman came up with the above conclusion in his 1982 paper "Simulating physics with computers". He visualised a machine capable of simulating quantum properties. Recent developments in the field of quantum mechanics raise the odds that a quantum computer can be built so that it is operates according to the theoretical model.

A quantum computer is a machine that uses the laws of quantum mechanics to perform calculations, ideally in a more efficient way than a classical computer.

The basic unit of quantum information is the qubit. Its basis states are:
       | 0 >   and   |1 >         to account for the 0 and 1 bits in classical information.
Qubits are represented by quantum particles like photons and electrons, whereas their states are the possible values of some physical property like the electron spin (positive or negative).

The advantage of using a qubit rather than a bit is that a qubit initially exists as a superposition of both its basis states. When measured, the qubit will adopt a single value randomly, according to the probability associated with each of its basis states.
e.g. |qubit > = a | 0 > + b | 1 >
where a and b are probability amplitudes satisfying |a|2 + |b|2 = 1
This allows a quantum computer to carry out a task with many inputs in parallel.

A quantum computer's memory consists of qubits (|0> , |1>, or superposition states). The memory itself is a superposition of all these qubits. For example, consider a register (collection of qubits) consisting of 3 qubits. The superposition of all possible states is:
|state_of_register > = a|000> + b| 001> + c|010> + d|011> + e|100> + f|101> + g|110> + h|111>
where letters a to h represent amplitude probabilities.


i.e. while a classical 3-bit register can store just one number between 0 and 7, a quantum 3-qubit register can store all numbers between this range. An n-qubit sized register can therefore store up to 2n states simultaneously.
The benefit of using a quantum computer is that it performs operations on all these states in only one computation step. As a result, the operations use fewer resources and they are carried out faster.


A quantum computer can solve some problems faster than a classical one. An example of such problems is the capability to produce the prime factors of larger and larger numbers in a reasonably finite time. In quantum computing this can be done in polynomial time using Shor's algorithm.

When an algorithm used by the quantum computer terminates, a measurement is made. In quantum computing measurement is an attempt to convert the quantum information of a system into classical information (i.e. bits). During this process the superposition randomly reduces to a single state. The value of this final state (i.e. the result of the algorithm) is then output to the user, but in the meantime the initial superposition state has been destroyed.

Quantum computing makes use of entanglement too. When two or more qubits form an entangled state, the measurement of one qubit causes a correlated measurement on the other qubit(s) as well.

Differences between classical and quantum computer:

ClassicalQuantum
Recourse: bitResource: bit, qubit, ebit (bit of entanglement, eg when A and B share an entangled
pair of qubits)
A bit represents the value 0 or 1A qubit represents the value 0, 1 or any superposition of both these values
Information can be copiedNo-cloning theorem: no machine can possibly produce an identical copy of a qubit

More applications:
  • Quantum cryptography - Bennett and Brassard, 1984. They created the BB84 protocol regarding quantum key distribution. The use of this quantum protocol makes unauthorised intervention practically impossible to hide.
  • Quantum teleportation - Uses entanglement to transfer one qubit of quantum information from origin A to destination B. Origin qubit is destroyed in the process.

Like everything, quantum computers have drawbacks too:
  • Measurement is irreversible: when we make a measurement on the state of the computer we destroy it; we cannot do any subsequent measurements based on the initial data.
  • Measurement is partial: according to the uncertainty principle, we cannot know every property of a particle with 100% accuracy. Therefore, measuring the state of a quantum computer may not produce an absolutely representative output. Indeed, many algorithms generate the correct answer with a certain probability.
  • Decoherence: the quantum computer must be completely isolated from the surroundings as the interaction with other microscopic objects will destroy its superposition.
