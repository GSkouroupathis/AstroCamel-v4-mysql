Schrodinger’s cat

Source: Wikipedia

Schrodinger’s cat is a thought experiment formed by Austrian physicist Erwin Schrodinger in 1935. In this experiment a cat is thought to be in a closed box along with a flask containing poison. The poison will be released according to some random event. In this case, the poison will be released and kill the cat when radioactivity is recorded to be present in the box (given out by the radioactive source that is also placed in the box). According to the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics, one cannot know if the cat is alive or dead with certainty; the cat is said to be simultaneously alive and dead. Such state has never been observed!

Schrodinger’s thought experiment raises the question of when does a quantum system changes its state from superposition to a single classical one. If we accept the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics then we can say that the cat stops being in both Alive and Dead states when an observation / measurement takes place, i.e. when someone opens the box and observes the cat. But what is no one opens the box or performs any other similar observation anytime soon? Will the cat still be in a mixture of states? Can we ever describe with certainty the health of the cat?

The above argument causes justified questions about the ‘logic’ of quantum mechanics in terms of common sense. Some may say that the quantum theory is false, buggy or just crazy. However, one must consider quantum decoherence. This theory says that the wave function (i.e. the overall state) of a macroscopic system may be influenced by the environment in such a way that causes it to collapse, thus becoming a single classical state. Therefore, in the case of the cat, the interaction of the external environment with the cat to adopt a single state only, Alive or Dead, without involving any measurements. So, on this view, superpositions exist even in the macroscopic world but, due to decoherence, they cannot be observed.

The Schrodinger’s cat paradox leads to a very important conclusion regarding quantum logic: OUR EVERYDAY (MACROSCOPIC) WORLD IS CLASSICAL. Quantum logic did not replace the logic that was established centuries ago, but rather it enforced some constraints on it regarding the microscopic world.
