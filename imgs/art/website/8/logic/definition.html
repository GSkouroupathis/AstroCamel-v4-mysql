Quantum logic: Definition

What is quantum logic?

Quantum logic is a set of rules that allows us to reason about propositions in a quantum context. These rules, unlike the rules of classical logic, take the principles of quantum theory into consideration.

But first of all, what do propositions have to do with the rules of quantum mechanics? What do we mean by the phrase "a formula A holds?" The answer is: a physical measurement about a quantity A which results in the value μ asserts that the proposition A = μ is true. This is an important connection between physics and logic; making a measurement is analogous to asserting a corresponding proposition.

In quantum mechanics the state of a system is determined by its wave function. According to Birkhoff and von Neumann the state should thus be represented by a function / phase space, namely the Hilbert space (an infinite-dimensional vector space with an inner product). Every proposition in logic is then associated with a closed subspace of the Hilbert space; we say that the proposition is projected onto this space. The truth value of the proposition is determined by the eigenvalues of its projector on that space.

The collection of all projectors on the Hilbert space forms a specific lattice on which the operators join (U), meet (∩) and complement (⊥) correspond to the logical connectives disjunction (V), conjunction (∧) and negation (~) respectively.

One of the most important aspects of quantum logic is the failure of the classical distributive law. Because of this, a weaker version of the distributive law (called the modular law) was developed to take the place of its classical predecessor.

When using quantum logic to make inferences, one must be extremely careful on how he uses the specified propositions. Consider this simple example in natural deduction:

  • A                given
  • B                given
  • C                given
  • A ∧ B      ∧I (1,2)

In classical logic the above example would be perfectly OK: in any system where the propositions A, B and C hold we can deduce that A ∧ B holds. In quantum logic however, if we want to deduce the same conclusion then we must consider the extra assumption that there exist no uncertainty relations between A, B and C (i.e. knowing B for example does not invalidate what we previously knew about A).
